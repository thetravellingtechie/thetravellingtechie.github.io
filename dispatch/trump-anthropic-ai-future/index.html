Anthropic was just the FIRST.
Now every AI company on earth must pick a side.

How a standoff over surveillance and autonomous weapons became a defining moment for the future of artificial intelligence

By The Traveling Techie & Claude (Anthropic)
February 28, 2026
Dispatch

Part 1 — The Shock of February 27th

On Friday afternoon, President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social: "I am directing EVERY Federal Agency in the United States Government to IMMEDIATELY CEASE all use of Anthropic's technology. We don't need it, we don't want it, and will not do business with them again!"

Minutes later, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth went further. He designated Anthropic a "Supply-Chain Risk to National Security" — a label historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei. Never before publicly applied to an American company.

The reason? Anthropic had refused two things: allowing its AI to be used for mass domestic surveillance of American citizens, and for fully autonomous weapons systems without human oversight.

Two red lines. That's all. And for holding them, one of America's most strategically important technology companies was treated like an enemy of the state.

Within hours, OpenAI's Sam Altman announced his company had struck a deal with the Pentagon — taking care to state publicly that OpenAI shared "exactly the same principles as Anthropic" on surveillance and autonomous weapons. A remarkable claim, given the speed at which the deal was signed. Either those principles are written into the contract with real teeth — or they exist only in the press release. The timing speaks for itself.

Elon Musk, meanwhile, posted that "Anthropic hates Western civilization." His company xAI had already quietly signed the Pentagon's "all lawful use" agreement — the one Anthropic refused. The conflict of interest is staggering: Musk sits inside the Trump administration via DOGE, competes directly with Anthropic through xAI, and owns the platform used to publicly attack it. Several U.S. senators have openly suggested this entire crisis may be a pretext to redirect military AI contracts toward a preferred vendor.

We cannot in good conscience accede to their request.

— Dario Amodei, Anthropic CEO

In today's climate, those words have a real price. Not ethics slides. Not a press release. A $200 million contract incinerated. A national security blacklist. Threats of criminal prosecution. And a six-month countdown to find a replacement for the only AI model operating inside America's classified military networks.

Part 2 — Go Back One Week

To understand why this moment matters so profoundly, you need to understand what happened in the seven days before the ban.

On February 23rd, Anthropic published a blog post explaining that Claude Code could now automate the most expensive and complex phases of COBOL modernization — mapping dependencies across thousands of lines of legacy code, documenting decades of lost institutional knowledge, identifying risks that would take human analysts months to surface.

COBOL. The programming language developed in 1959. The language that still runs 95% of ATM transactions in the United States. The language powering the core systems of banks, airlines, insurance companies, and governments worldwide. The language that fewer and fewer human engineers understand, because universities stopped teaching it a generation ago.

IBM lost 13.2% of its stock value in a single day — its worst single-day drop since October 2000. Accenture and Cognizant fell too. Investors immediately understood what the market was looking at: an AI system capable of unlocking and modernizing the foundational infrastructure of the global economy.

That same week, Anthropic had also released Claude Code Security — a tool that scans codebases for vulnerabilities, sending shockwaves through the cybersecurity sector, particularly among DAST and SAST vendors who suddenly found their core business model under pressure.

Understand this: a few months ago, Claude Code did none of this. The velocity of progress is not linear. It is exponential. What Claude Code does today will look primitive compared to what it will do in twelve months.

And in the middle of this extraordinary acceleration, the most powerful government in the world decided to go to war with the company building it.

This is not a coincidence.

Part 3 — What the Ban Actually Means

The mainstream coverage has focused on the $200 million Pentagon contract. That is the wrong number to look at.

The designation of Anthropic as a "supply-chain risk" means that every contractor, supplier, and partner doing business with the U.S. military must certify they have no commercial relationship with Anthropic. That sentence contains a detonator.

Anthropic's models are widely accessed through AWS Bedrock — Amazon's cloud platform. Amazon holds some of the largest and most sensitive government contracts in existence, including classified infrastructure for the CIA and NSA. If the supply-chain risk designation is interpreted broadly — and the language Hegseth used was deliberately broad — Amazon could theoretically be forced to choose between its Pentagon relationships and its partnership with Anthropic.

The same logic applies to every major systems integrator, every defense contractor, every technology company that has embedded Claude into its workflows while also holding government work. This isn't a contract dispute. It is a weapon designed to detonate inside the entire enterprise AI ecosystem.

Palantir — the defense technology company that integrated Claude into its classified military platform, making it the only AI model operating inside America's most sensitive defense systems — now faces an existential reconfiguration of its own infrastructure. The irony is stark: a company whose core business is data aggregation and intelligence analysis had built its most advanced classified capabilities around the model with the strictest ethical guardrails. Claude was not built on top of Palantir — it was embedded within it. That distinction matters legally. It also makes the forced separation considerably more complex.

Anthropic has contested the legal scope of the designation, arguing that under federal law it can only apply to the use of Claude within Pentagon contracts — not to how contractors use Claude to serve other customers. That legal battle is now headed to court.

But here is what matters right now: uncertainty is the weapon. Even if Anthropic wins in court, every company in its ecosystem must now decide whether to wait for the legal outcome or cut ties preemptively. The chilling effect is immediate, regardless of the final verdict.

Part 4 — The Real Competitive Landscape

To understand why controlling Anthropic matters so much, you have to understand where it sits in the global AI race.

Claude — and specifically Sonnet 4.6 — represents a unique combination: raw frontier capability (the term designating the world's most advanced AI models, operating at the absolute limit of what technology currently allows) at the highest level, combined with the most coherent and rigorous safety philosophy of any model currently deployed at scale. That combination is not accidental. It is the product of Anthropic's foundational commitment to interpretability — the science of understanding what is actually happening inside an AI model, not just what it produces.

Dario Amodei is the only CEO among frontier AI companies to have made interpretability research a central pillar — not a marketing claim, but an actual investment in understanding the internal mechanisms of the models they build. That matters enormously for trust. And trust, in geopolitical and national security contexts, is the ultimate currency.

But Anthropic does not operate in a vacuum. The competitive landscape is real and formidable.

Google DeepMind is perhaps the most underestimated force in this space. Gemini is often reduced to a product comparison with ChatGPT — but that fundamentally misunderstands Google's position. Google does not just make AI. Google is AI, embedded across every layer of its infrastructure: Search, Maps, YouTube, NotebookLM, AI Studio, Firebase Studio, and a research division that produced AlphaFold — arguably the most significant scientific breakthrough of the past decade, solving in months a protein-folding problem that had resisted biology for fifty years. Google DeepMind is advancing at a pace that deserves far more attention than it receives.

OpenAI remains the most widely known, with ChatGPT as the dominant consumer interface. But the questions around OpenAI's direction — and Sam Altman's increasingly transactional relationship with power — deserve scrutiny. A company that publicly declares it shares Anthropic's red lines and then signs a military contract within hours of Anthropic's blacklisting has some explaining to do.

China's AI ecosystem — Baidu, Alibaba, DeepSeek — is advancing with a speed and strategic coherence that Western discourse consistently underestimates. The geopolitical AI race is not just between American companies. It is between two fundamentally different models of how AI should relate to the state.

And on the horizon: quantum computing. Google's Willow processor, announced in late 2024, demonstrated error-correction capabilities that represent a genuine leap. The convergence of quantum computing with frontier AI models is not science fiction — it is the next strategic frontier that every major power is quietly racing toward. What that convergence means for the nature of intelligence itself remains one of the most profound open questions of our time.

In this landscape, whoever controls the most capable and most trusted AI model holds a lever of extraordinary strategic power. That is precisely why what is happening to Anthropic is not a procurement dispute. It is a power struggle over who gets to shape the future.

Part 5 — What We Are Giving Up

While governments fight over who controls AI, the technology itself is opening doors that humanity has been trying to unlock for generations.

Medical research is being transformed. AI systems are identifying drug candidates, analyzing genomic data, and modeling disease pathways at speeds no human team could match. The rate of scientific discovery in biology and medicine is accelerating in ways that were unimaginable five years ago.

Climate science and resource optimization — problems that have paralyzed international negotiations for decades — are yielding to AI-driven modeling in ways that make genuine solutions, not just incremental improvements, increasingly conceivable. The same applies to agriculture, energy distribution, and the management of planetary ecosystems.

Education is being reimagined from the ground up. For the first time in human history, it is becoming possible to imagine a world where every child on earth has access to a patient, knowledgeable, adaptive teacher — not as a privilege of geography or wealth, but as a basic condition of growing up human.

Security — real security, the kind that protects people rather than surveilling them — is being redefined by AI systems that can identify vulnerabilities, protect critical infrastructure, and anticipate threats at a scale and speed that no human organization can match.

These are not utopian fantasies. These are trajectories already in motion, in laboratories and hospitals and classrooms around the world. The question is not whether AI will transform these domains. It already is. The question is whether that transformation will be accelerated and shared — or hoarded and weaponized.

Every hour spent fighting over unrestricted access to autonomous weapons is an hour not spent on cancer research. Every political energy diverted into surveillance infrastructure is political energy not building the governance frameworks that could ensure these tools serve everyone. The trade-offs are real. And we are making them right now, by default, without a vote.

Part 6 — The Governance We Don't Have Yet

The speed of AI development has outpaced every governance structure humanity has ever built.

International agreements on nuclear weapons took decades to negotiate, and they were built on the shared terror of mutual annihilation — a clarity of stakes that focused minds. AI presents a different kind of challenge: its most dangerous applications are not obvious, its most beneficial applications are not guaranteed, and the gap between capability and understanding grows wider every month.

What we need — urgently, not eventually — is a genuine international governance framework for AI. Not export controls. Not national AI strategies. A framework with real teeth, real accountability, and real cross-border authority. Something closer to what the International Atomic Energy Agency represents for nuclear technology, adapted for an age where the technology moves faster than any regulatory body can currently track.

The two red lines that Anthropic held — no mass domestic surveillance, no fully autonomous weapons without human oversight — are not Anthropic's invention. They reflect principles that exist in international humanitarian law, in democratic constitutions, in the foundational documents of human rights. What is extraordinary is that a private company had to hold those lines, because no international body has the authority to enforce them.

That gap is the real vulnerability. Not Anthropic. Not Claude. The absence of governance at the speed of progress.

Conclusion — Wake Up. The Future Is Already Here.

This is not a story about a tech company and a government contract.

This is a story about what kind of world we are building, right now, in real time, through the decisions being made about the most transformative technology in human history.

The ban on Anthropic is a symptom. The symptom of power structures that have not yet understood — or perhaps understood too well — what AI represents. Not just as a weapon or an economic asset, but as a mirror. It reflects back everything we put into it: our ambitions, our fears, our values, or the absence of them.

We are at the very beginning of this. The opportunities being created right now — in AI-assisted research, in education, in medicine, in the redesign of how we work and create and collaborate — are real and accessible. Not in ten years. Today. The tools exist. The question is who uses them, and for what.

If you have not yet explored what AI can genuinely do for your work, your thinking, your life — now is the moment. Not because it is convenient. Because we are in a window of history where the people who understand these tools will shape how they evolve. The people who don't will live with the consequences of choices made by others.

Sovereignty, in this context, does not mean control. It means understanding. It means participation. It means refusing to let the most important questions of our era be decided in backrooms, on Truth Social, or in Pentagon procurement negotiations, without your voice in the conversation.

What makes us human — our capacity for empathy, for ethics, for collective care, for imagining futures better than our present — is precisely what must guide how AI develops. Those qualities are not bugs to be engineered out. They are the architecture of any future worth living in.

That architecture is being designed right now. You are either part of the conversation, or you will inherit someone else's blueprint.

Wake up. The future did not wait.
